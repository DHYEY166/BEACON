RED CROSS FIRST AID MANUAL — FIELD RESPONDER EDITION

APPROACH TO ANY EMERGENCY — PRIMARY SURVEY
Step 1: Ensure scene safety — look for hazards before approaching. Do not become a casualty yourself. Step 2: Assess responsiveness — tap shoulders gently and ask loudly are you okay. Step 3: Call for help — shout for assistance, send bystander for medical support. Step 4: Airway — open with head-tilt chin-lift or jaw thrust. Step 5: Breathing — look, listen, feel for 10 seconds. Step 6: Circulation — check for pulse and severe bleeding. Step 7: Disability — assess level of consciousness using AVPU scale. Step 8: Expose — remove clothing to assess injuries but prevent hypothermia.

AVPU SCALE FOR CONSCIOUSNESS
A — Alert: patient is awake and responds normally. V — Voice: patient responds when spoken to loudly. P — Pain: patient responds only to painful stimulus such as sternal rub. U — Unresponsive: patient does not respond to any stimulus. Any patient below Alert level needs urgent assessment and likely referral.

CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION — ADULT
Check for responsiveness. Call for help. Open airway head-tilt chin-lift. Check breathing for no more than 10 seconds. If not breathing or only gasping begin CPR. 30 chest compressions — push hard and fast, at least 5cm depth, allow full recoil, 100 to 120 per minute. 2 rescue breaths — tilt head, lift chin, pinch nose, seal mouth, breathe until chest rises, one second per breath. Continue 30-2 ratio until AED available, patient starts breathing, or rescuer exhausted. If unable to give rescue breaths do compression-only CPR continuously.

CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION — CHILD
Under 8 years: use one hand for chest compressions, compress one third of chest depth. 30 compressions then 2 rescue breaths if alone, 15 compressions then 2 rescue breaths if two rescuers. Infant under 1 year: two fingers on centre of chest, compress one third chest depth. Cover both nose and mouth with rescuer mouth for breaths. Start CPR with 5 rescue breaths before compressions for children.

CHOKING — CONSCIOUS ADULT
Ask is the person choking. If they can cough encourage them to keep coughing. If they cannot cough or speak or are turning blue — 5 back blows between shoulder blades with heel of hand, then 5 abdominal thrusts Heimlich manoeuvre pushing in and up under ribcage. Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until object expelled or person becomes unconscious. If unconscious start CPR and look in mouth before each rescue breath.

CHOKING — INFANT
Hold infant face down along forearm supporting head. Give 5 back blows with heel of hand between shoulder blades. Turn infant face up. Give 5 chest thrusts with two fingers on breastbone one finger breadth below nipple line. Check mouth after each set — remove object only if clearly visible. Do not perform blind finger sweeps. If infant becomes unconscious start infant CPR.

UNCONSCIOUS BREATHING PATIENT
Place in recovery position: kneel beside patient, place far arm at right angle to body, bring near arm across chest, bend far knee up, roll patient away from you onto their side, adjust top leg to right angle, tilt head back to maintain airway, monitor breathing continuously. Check position every 30 minutes if left in recovery position.

SEVERE ALLERGIC REACTION — ANAPHYLAXIS
Signs: sudden onset skin rash hives, swelling face lips tongue throat, difficulty breathing wheezing, severe drop in blood pressure, vomiting. If epinephrine adrenaline auto-injector available: inject into outer thigh, can inject through clothing, hold for 3 seconds, massage injection site. Lay person flat and raise legs unless breathing difficulty then semi-sit. Call for emergency services. Second dose of epinephrine can be given after 5 to 15 minutes if no improvement. Do not give oral medications to person with swollen throat.

DIABETIC EMERGENCIES
Low blood sugar hypoglycaemia: pale, sweating, shaking, confusion, headache, weakness. Person must be conscious and able to swallow. Give 15 to 20 grams of fast-acting sugar — 3 to 4 glucose tablets, half cup of fruit juice, half cup regular soft drink not diet. Recheck after 15 minutes. If no improvement give another dose. Once recovered give complex carbohydrate snack. High blood sugar hyperglycaemia: gradual onset, excessive thirst and urination, fruity breath, nausea. Conscious patient can drink water. Refer for assessment.

STROKE RECOGNITION — FAST
F — Face: ask person to smile — does one side droop. A — Arms: ask person to raise both arms — does one drift down. S — Speech: ask person to repeat a phrase — is speech slurred or strange. T — Time: if any of these signs are present note time and seek emergency care immediately. Additional signs: sudden severe headache, sudden vision changes, sudden dizziness or loss of balance. Do not give aspirin unless certain diagnosis of ischaemic stroke. Lay person on affected side if drooling.

BLEEDING CONTROL — EXTERNAL
Direct pressure with clean cloth for minimum 10 minutes without lifting. Elevate injured part above heart level unless fracture suspected. If blood soaks through do not remove cloth — add more on top and continue pressure. Tourniquet for life-threatening limb bleeding not controlled by direct pressure: apply 5 to 7cm above wound, tighten until bleeding stops, note time of application, do not remove. Wound packing for junctional bleeding groin armpit neck where tourniquet cannot be applied: pack gauze firmly into wound, maintain firm constant pressure.

INTERNAL BLEEDING RECOGNITION
Suspect internal bleeding after: blunt abdominal trauma, high-speed accident, fall from height, assault. Signs: rapid weak pulse, low blood pressure, pale cold clammy skin, abdominal rigidity or distension, bruising over abdomen or flanks, blood in urine or vomit or stool. Do not give food or fluids. Lay flat, keep warm. Urgent evacuation to surgical facility. Monitor vital signs every 5 to 10 minutes.

FRACTURES AND DISLOCATIONS
Assess before and after treatment: pulse, sensation, movement distal to injury. Immobilise in position found — do not straighten unless distal circulation absent and medical care more than one hour away. Improvised splint should extend beyond joints above and below fracture. Pad bony prominences. Check circulation every 15 minutes after splinting. Elevate if possible. Open fractures: cover wound with clean moist dressing before splinting. Never attempt to reduce a dislocation without proper training and pain control.

CRUSH INJURIES
Do not release a crushed limb without medical preparation if crush has lasted more than 15 minutes. Releasing a long-crushed limb can cause crush syndrome — potassium and myoglobin released into circulation causing cardiac arrest and kidney failure. Give IV fluids before and during release if available. Apply tourniquet before release if crush over 15 minutes and facilities unavailable. Transport urgently after release.

AMPUTATION MANAGEMENT
Control bleeding with direct pressure and tourniquet. Wrap amputated part in clean damp cloth — do not put directly on ice. Place wrapped part in plastic bag, put bag in water with ice if available. Do not scrub or clean amputated part. Transport part with patient to facility.

EYE INJURIES
Chemical splash: immediately irrigate with large amounts of clean water for 15 to 20 minutes, hold eyelids open, remove contact lenses if possible. Foreign body: do not rub eye, pull upper eyelid over lower to dislodge, flush with clean water. Penetrating injury: do not remove object, cover with clean protective covering without pressure, cover other eye to reduce movement. Blunt injury: apply cold compress, seek assessment for internal injury.

DENTAL EMERGENCIES
Knocked out adult tooth: handle by crown not root, rinse gently with milk or saline, reinsert in socket if possible or store in milk, seek dental care within 30 minutes. Dental abscess: swelling spreading to neck or floor of mouth is emergency — airway may be compromised. Give paracetamol for pain. Antibiotics amoxicillin if spreading infection. Refer for drainage.

EAR AND NOSE EMERGENCIES
Nosebleed: sit patient upright leaning slightly forward, pinch soft part of nose for 15 minutes without releasing, apply ice pack to bridge of nose. Do not tilt head back — blood may be swallowed. Insect in ear: do not probe, tilt head to affected side, try to flush with clean water. Suspected perforated eardrum from blast or injury — do not irrigate, cover loosely, refer.

HEAT EXHAUSTION
Heavy sweating, weakness, cold pale clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, fainting. Move to cool environment, lay person down and elevate legs, loosen tight clothing, apply cool wet cloths, give cool water to sip if conscious. Should improve within 30 minutes. If condition does not improve or worsens suspect heatstroke.

HEATSTROKE
High body temperature above 39.4 degrees, hot red dry or damp skin, rapid strong pulse, confusion or unconsciousness. This is a life-threatening emergency. Call for emergency care immediately. Move to cool environment, cool rapidly by any means available — cool wet sheets, fanning, cool water sponging, ice packs to neck armpits groin. Do not give fluids if altered consciousness. Target temperature reduction to below 39 degrees within 30 minutes.

HYPOTHERMIA FIELD MANAGEMENT
Mild hypothermia: shivering, confusion, slow clumsy movements. Move to shelter, remove wet clothing, insulate from ground, apply warm packs to armpits groin neck, give warm sweet drinks if fully conscious. Moderate to severe hypothermia: little or no shivering, rigid muscles, very slow or absent pulse, unconscious. Handle very gently — cold heart is irritable and rough movement can cause cardiac arrest. Keep horizontal. Warm core gently do not rub limbs. Do not give drinks. Transport carefully to warm facility.

WATER SAFETY AND DROWNING
Drowning rescue: do not enter water unless trained. Throw a rope, branch, clothing, or buoyancy aid. Only enter water as last resort with assistance. After rescue: if person not breathing begin CPR immediately. Do not waste time attempting to drain water — it does not significantly obstruct airway. Drowning persons should be assessed at facility even if apparently recovered due to delayed pulmonary oedema.

ANIMAL BITES AND RABIES PREVENTION
Wash bite wound thoroughly with soap and water for 15 minutes immediately — this is the single most effective intervention. Irrigate with clean water under pressure. Apply antiseptic if available. Report to health facility for rabies post-exposure prophylaxis assessment. High-risk bites: bat bites, any bite on face head or neck, multiple bites, unprovoked attack. Do not delay PEP while awaiting animal observation — begin immediately for high-risk bites.

POISONING — GENERAL APPROACH
Remove from source if safe. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically advised by poison centre — corrosives and petroleum products cause more damage on the way back up. Give activated charcoal if available and advised, patient conscious and able to swallow, within 1 hour of ingestion. Bring substance container or sample to hospital. Organophosphate poisoning signs: excessive secretions, small pupils, muscle twitching, breathing difficulty — give atropine if available.

TRIAGE IN MULTIPLE CASUALTY SITUATIONS
Simple triage and rapid treatment START method. Walking wounded — direct to collecting area, tag green. Not walking — assess breathing. Not breathing — open airway, if not breathing tag black. Breathing — assess respiratory rate. Over 30 per minute tag red. Under 30 per minute — assess pulse or capillary refill. No pulse or refill over 2 seconds tag red. Pulse present and refill under 2 seconds — assess mental status. Cannot follow commands tag red. Can follow commands tag yellow.

DOCUMENTATION IN THE FIELD
Document for every patient: name or description, age estimated if not known, time of assessment, vital signs pulse rate and character, respiratory rate, temperature if measured, level of consciousness AVPU, main complaint or injury, treatment given and time, response to treatment, plan and next assessment time. Triage tag if multiple casualties — attach to patient and do not remove. Incident log — date time location type of incident number treated referrals made.
